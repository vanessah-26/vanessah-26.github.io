Resonance
Music & Multilingual Poetry Discovery
KeyBERT · FastAPI · ChromaDB · HuggingFace · Docker

Overview

Resonance is a cross-modal NLP system that bridges classical music and poetry through semantic matching and RAG. The user selects a musical piece; the system retrieves the two most relevant poems via semantic matching from a multilingual corpus that includes English, Vietnamese, Mandarin, and French.

The project aims to explore how non-lyrical music can be experienced through another medium, such as poetry, and how the same semantic tagging techniques behind platforms like Medium, Spotify, Netflix, etc can be built without manual labeling by creators.

Problem

As digital entertainment and content creations become easier to access, many influential media such as classical music and poetry are losing relevance with general audiences.

Although non-lyrical music is meant to be felt, not decoded, without context, it can feel distant to those without a formal background. Additionally, poems that shaped literary traditions and generations across centuries sit largely undiscovered.

Resonance invites audience to rediscover familiar musical pieces with poems from diverse culture and traditions, offering new emotional lenses and interpretation to this experience.

How it works

Select any classical music piece from the dropdown. Click Find Matching Poems and Resonance returns two poems that share the same emotional mood and tone as the music, drawn from a multilingual corpus across English, Vietnamese, Mandarin, and French. Each poem shows a match score so you can see how closely it aligns.

Not feeling the results? Hit Find Matching Poems again. The system remembers what it already showed you and surfaces the next best matches, letting you explore the corpus deeper with every click.

Product flow

Technical design decisions

1. Automate poem tags

To create tags for poems, KeyBERT was used to embed the full texts of each poem, then embed each word individually. The word vector will be compared with the poem vector through cosine similarity to find the words that are most similar to the poem, or represent the poem the best. After that, apply the Maximal Marginal Relevance (MMR) method to ensure that the most relevant words are closest to the poem, but also not redundant. For instance, if the poems have multiple similar words such as “moon”, “moonlight”, “moonlit”, etc. Then they essentially share the same meaning, MMR ensures to pick the most relevant word among the three without taking another similar word to represent the poem.

Then, map each of these raw keywords to a MOOD_MAP to finalize the mood or tone of the poem. For instance, raw words like “tree” can represent “nature”, “love” can represent “romantic”, “lonely” can represent “sorrow” or “melancholy”. This step used a rule-based lexicon to finalize these tags. Without this step, the raw words might not be the best version to represent the theme or the mood of the poem. Adding this step also results in improving the matching performance for the music and poem relevance.

2. RAG implementation

This step uses a Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) method to match music pieces to poems, rank the relevant poems, and provide the two highest-matching poems at every query.

Each music piece has pre-computed features such as tempo, key, mood, and character that are stored in sheets_metadata.json. At query time, when the user clicks “Find Matching Poems”, the system will embed tags of the selected music piece using SentenceTransformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) into a 384-dimensional vector. ChromaDB then performs cosine similarity search across all 61 tag vectors of all poems and returns the closest matches. The music and poem tags are embedded using the same model, which helps in the retrieval step since they’re in a shared vector space.

Tradeoffs

Adding a session-based exclusion mechanism prevents repetitive poems across multiple music pieces. When user reruns to find new matching poems, the repeated queries surface different poems and produce the next two matches. However, the cost is the highest-scoring poem in the corpus sometimes never appears for a certain music piece because it was already paired with another piece.

This tradeoff prioritizes poems exploration and relevant matches. The best matching poems (90%+) might not be shown but users can discover more results, which is the main goal of the tool.

Outcome

The system consistently returns the top 2 matching poems for each selection and the next 2 best results at every rerun.

Reflection

What I’d do differently: I’d invest earlier in corpus signal quality rather than expanding the poem count. The bottleneck was always the MOOD_MAP translation layer, not how many poems were in the corpus. Better-tagged poems would have improved retrieval more than adding volume.

Future improvements

  • Add upload feature for users to upload a music sheet, the app will extract, match, and provide high-matching poems
  • Add text-to-speech for non-English poems
  • Expand music and poems corpora
  • The architecture generalizes to any domain where content is rich in semantic meaning or can be translated into semantic form
